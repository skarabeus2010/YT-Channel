## VO — Kapitel (EN, Urfassung 8 Kap. — Referenz; tatsächliche Produktion = 17 Kap. siehe oben)
### CH1 Hook
> "Two and a half thousand years ago, in one Greek city, ordinary men did something no one had really done before: they ruled themselves. No king, no emperor — the people, deciding by vote. This is a single day in ancient Athens, the city that invented democracy, in all its brilliance and all its contradictions."

### CH2 The morning
> "Our Athenian wakes in a modest mud-brick house and steps into the heart of the city: the agora. It's marketplace, news feed, and meeting place all at once — stalls of figs, fish, and pottery, money-changers, gossip, and philosophers arguing in the colonnades. To be a citizen here is not to stay home and mind your business. It is to show up, in public, and take part."

### CH3 Democracy in action
> "Today the Assembly is meeting. Our citizen climbs the Pnyx, a bare hill near the Acropolis, where thousands of men gather in the open air. There are no professional politicians ruling them. Any citizen can stand on the speaker's platform and argue; then everyone votes, by a show of hands, on war, on laws, on money. Many public officials are chosen not by election but by lottery — because the Athenians believed that ruling was the right of every citizen, not just the clever or the rich."

### CH4 Who counted
> "But we have to be honest about this democracy. 'The people' did not mean everyone. Only free adult men born of Athenian parents could vote — perhaps ten to twenty percent of the population. Women had no political voice and were largely confined to the home. Foreigners, even long-settled ones, were shut out. And underneath it all was slavery: tens of thousands of enslaved people whose labour gave citizens the free time to do politics. Athenian freedom was real — and it was built on the unfreedom of others."

### CH5 Philosophy in the streets
> "Athens didn't just invent voting. In these same streets, a stonemason's son named Socrates is stopping people to ask maddening questions — what is justice? what is courage? — and refusing to accept easy answers. Around him, Western philosophy is being born in real time. Learning here isn't locked in libraries; it happens out loud, in the open, in argument."

### CH6 The theatre
> "A few times a year, the whole city pours into a great open-air theatre on the slope of the Acropolis. Tens of thousands watch plays that are part entertainment, part religion, part politics — tragedies that stage the deepest human questions, and comedies that savagely mock the very politicians sitting in the audience. For Athenians, going to the theatre was a civic duty, not just a night out."

### CH7 The symposium
> "As night falls, the wealthier citizen reclines at a symposium — a drinking party. Wine, always mixed with water, flows as men talk philosophy, recite poetry, sing, and argue late into the night. It is exclusive and male, but it is also the engine room of Athenian culture: ideas, jokes, and politics, fermenting together over the cups."

### CH8 Glory and shadow
> "This was the Golden Age — the age of the Parthenon, of drama, of philosophy that still shapes how we think. And yet the same democracy that gave the world a new idea of freedom also kept slaves, silenced women, and, in the end, voted to execute Socrates for asking too many questions. Athens is not a perfect ancestor. It is a mirror — showing us both how far an idea can reach, and how far it still has to go. Follow Embered Ages for more of the ancient world, brought to life by firelight."
