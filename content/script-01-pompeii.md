## Narration (VO, EN) — mit Kapiteln & [VISUAL]-Notizen

### 00:00 — The Last Normal Morning
> "This man has no idea that the city around him is about to vanish from the face of the earth — and that, almost two thousand years later, you will be looking at the bread he never got to sell."
`[VISUAL: Dawn over Pompeii's rooftops, Vesuvius calm in the haze behind. A baker opens his shop shutters. Slow push-in. Photorealistic, 35mm documentary.]`
> "This is Pompeii, a busy Roman town on the Bay of Naples. We don't know his name — so let's follow an ordinary baker through what no one yet knows is the city's final day. Historians can't tell us his exact story, but thanks to what the ash preserved, we can reconstruct his world in extraordinary detail."
`[VISUAL: Streets waking up — vendors, carts, morning light through narrow lanes.]`

### 01:20 — A City Frozen Mid-Sentence
> "Pompeii is not a ruin of empty temples. It's a snapshot of normal life. Archaeologists have found around thirty-five bakeries here. In one of them, eighty-one loaves were still sitting in the oven when the end came — round loaves, scored into segments like a pull-apart bread, some even stamped with the baker's name."
`[VISUAL: Carbonized round loaves in a stone oven; close macro of the baker's stamp. Warm interior light, flour dust.]`
> "Our baker sells to people on their way to work. And where do they eat? At one of roughly a hundred and fifty thermopolia — Rome's version of fast food. Stone counters set with deep jars kept stews, chickpeas and wine warm for the crowd."
`[VISUAL: A thermopolium counter with embedded dolia jars; customers leaning in; painted menu on the wall.]`
> "The walls themselves talk. They're covered in election posters, advertisements, and graffiti — jokes, insults, declarations of love. If you want to know what ordinary Romans actually thought, you read their walls."
`[VISUAL: Plastered wall with red-painted electoral notices and scratched graffiti; a hand trailing along it.]`

### 03:20 — The Mountain No One Feared
> "Behind all of this sits Vesuvius. But here's the strange part: the people of Pompeii didn't see it as a volcano at all. Its slopes were green with vineyards. There was no living memory of it ever erupting."
`[VISUAL: Vesuvius in the distance, lush vineyards on its lower slopes, farmers at work. Bright midday.]`
> "There had been a warning — of a kind. Seventeen years earlier, around 62 AD, a powerful earthquake had wrecked much of the town. In the year 79, Pompeii was still rebuilding. And in the days before the end, the ground trembled again. But the Roman writer Pliny the Younger tells us no one was alarmed — because tremors were simply common in this region."
`[VISUAL: Cracked walls under repair, scaffolding of timber, masons working. A faint tremor — dust trickling from a ceiling, a hanging lamp swaying.]`
> "Later Roman writers would claim there were omens — springs drying up, restless animals. But those stories were written long after the fact, by men who weren't there. On this morning, as far as anyone in Pompeii knew, it was just another ordinary day."

### 05:00 — The Cloud
> "Then, around one in the afternoon, it began."
`[VISUAL: Market square mid-day; people pause, look up. A low boom.]`
> "Across the bay, a teenager named Pliny the Younger watched a gigantic cloud rise over the mountain. He described it shaped like an umbrella pine — a tall trunk of ash climbing into the sky, then spreading into branches at the top. His account, written years later to the historian Tacitus, is the only eyewitness record of the disaster that survives."
`[VISUAL: The iconic umbrella-pine eruption column towering over Vesuvius, seen across the bay. Awe and dread.]`
> "His uncle, Pliny the Elder, commanded the Roman fleet nearby. He gave the order to sail toward the mountain — partly curiosity, partly to rescue people trapped along the coast. It was a decision that would cost him his life."
`[VISUAL: Roman warships launching from a harbor toward the smoking mountain, ash darkening the sky.]`

### 06:40 — A Rain of Stone
> "In Pompeii, the sky turned black at midday. Then the pumice began to fall — light, porous volcanic stone, raining down by the millions. At first it was a strange spectacle. Within hours, it was deadly."
`[VISUAL: Streets filling with grey pumice pellets, bouncing off roofs and pavement; people shielding their heads with pillows and cloth.]`
> "The stone piled up fast — knee-deep, then deeper. Roofs groaned under the weight and began to collapse. Our baker faces the choice that everyone in the city now faces: run, or shelter and wait it out."
`[VISUAL: A man tying a cushion to his head, gripping a lamp, hesitating in a doorway choked with falling ash. A roof beam cracks overhead.]`
> "Many fled toward the sea, stumbling through the dark with whatever they could carry. Others sealed themselves inside, hoping the rain of stone would simply stop. It did not."

### 08:10 — The Night the City Died
> "The truly lethal blow came later — in the night and the early morning. The towering column finally collapsed, and the mountain sent down pyroclastic surges: avalanches of superheated gas and ash moving faster than a person can run, hot enough to kill in an instant."
`[VISUAL: A glowing pyroclastic surge pouring down Vesuvius's flank toward the dark, buried town. Terrifying scale. NOTE: dramatized reconstruction.]`
> "By the time it was over, Pompeii had vanished under metres of ash — and stayed hidden for almost seventeen centuries."
`[VISUAL: Aftermath at dawn — a grey, featureless plain of ash where a city stood. Silence.]`
> "When archaeologists later dug it out, they found strange empty cavities in the hardened ash. In 1863, Giuseppe Fiorelli realised what they were: spaces left behind by bodies that had decayed away. By pouring plaster into the voids, he recovered the shapes of the people themselves — in the very positions they were in at the end. These casts are not a special effect. They are the closest thing we have to looking the disaster in the face."
`[VISUAL: A plaster cast in situ being revealed in grey ash; respectful, still framing. Museum lighting.]`

### 09:30 — What the Ash Kept
> "We will never know our baker's name, or whether he ran or stayed. That part is a reconstruction. But the bread in his oven is real. The graffiti on his walls is real. The fast-food counters, the half-rebuilt houses, the green slopes of a mountain nobody feared — all real, all preserved by the very thing that destroyed them."
`[VISUAL: Slow montage of the real preserved details — bread, walls, streets — intercut with the reconstructed living town. Warm, reflective.]`
> "Pompeii is a reminder that history isn't only kings and battles. Sometimes it's a single ordinary morning, frozen forever. If you want more of the ancient world brought back to life, follow along — there's a whole lost world waiting in the ash."
`[VISUAL: Final wide — reconstructed Pompeii alive at golden hour, Vesuvius quiet behind. Slow crane-up. Embered Ages end card.]`

## Fakt vs. Rekonstruktion (für Controller-Gate 6.2)
- **Fakt:** Bäckereien/81 Brote, thermopolia, Wand-Graffiti, 62-n.-Chr.-Beben, Plinius-Augenzeuge, ~13-Uhr-Wolke „Schirmpinie", Plinius d. Ä. fährt los & stirbt, Bimsstein→pyroklastische Ströme, Fiorelli-Abgüsse 1863.
- **Rekonstruktion (gekennzeichnet):** der namenlose Bäcker + sein Tagesablauf, alle bewegten Menschen-Szenen, die Surge-Visualisierung. VO sagt explizit „we don't know his name / that part is a reconstruction".
- **Bewusst entschärft:** Datum NICHT als „24. August" (offen: „around one in the afternoon", kein Kalendertag); Vorzeichen nur als „later writers would claim".
