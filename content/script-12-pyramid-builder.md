## VO — Kapitel (EN)
### CH1 Hook
> "You've been told the pyramids were built by slaves — whipped under the sun, dying in their thousands. It's one of the most famous images in history. And it's wrong. The people who raised the Great Pyramid were not slaves. They were skilled, paid Egyptians, fed and housed and cared for, who lived and died in the shadow of the monument they built. This is what a single day on the greatest building site of the ancient world was really like."

### CH2 Who they were
> "The myth of slave-built pyramids comes from Hollywood and from the Greek writer Herodotus, writing two thousand years after the fact. The evidence on the ground tells another story. Beside the pyramids, archaeologists found a whole town — bakeries, breweries, dormitories — and the cemeteries of the workers themselves. These were organised crews, free men who rotated in from across Egypt, grouped into teams with proud names like 'Friends of Khufu' and 'Drunkards of Menkaure.' They weren't dragged here. Building the king's tomb was a national project — and, for many, an honour."

### CH3 The morning
> "Our builder wakes before dawn in the workers' village. Breakfast is the fuel of the pyramid age: bread and beer. Egyptian beer was thick, nutritious, and mildly alcoholic — closer to a liquid loaf than a modern pint — and it was handed out as part of the daily ration. A skilled worker might receive bread, beer, onions, and fish. Records carved on site even track who showed up and who took a day off — for everything from brewing beer to a scorpion sting."

### CH4 The work
> "Then, the work. Teams of haulers drag two-and-a-half-ton limestone blocks on wooden sledges across the sand — pouring water ahead of the runners to cut the friction, a trick we can still see painted on Egyptian walls. Up vast earthen ramps the blocks climb, set in place by gangs working in rhythm. There was no single secret machine; the pyramid was built by organisation — thousands of people, moving millions of stones, over roughly twenty years."

### CH5 Food and medicine
> "These were valued workers, and it shows in their bones. Skeletons from the workers' cemetery reveal something remarkable: healed fractures, set with care, and even signs of surgery. Injured builders were treated, not discarded. Their diet included bread, beer, fish, vegetables, and — for the pyramid builders specifically — a steady supply of meat. Records suggest cattle, sheep, and goats were driven to the site to feed the crews. You do not feed prime beef to disposable slaves."

### CH6 Life and death
> "When a worker died on the job, he wasn't thrown into a pit. He was buried in his own tomb, in a cemetery right beside the pyramids of the kings he served — stocked with bread and beer for the afterlife. That single fact undoes the slavery myth. Slaves are not buried in honour, in the holiest ground in Egypt, equipped for eternity. These men believed they were helping their god-king reach the heavens — and that they would share in that journey."

### CH7 Who really built them
> "So who built the pyramids? Egyptians. A rotating workforce of farmers, craftsmen, and labourers, organised by a powerful state. Many likely worked during the season when the Nile flooded their fields and farming stopped — turning idle months into a national effort, fed and paid by the crown. It was perhaps the world's first great public-works project: not a monument to cruelty, but to organisation, faith, and astonishing collective will."

### CH8 Legacy
> "The slave myth survives because it's dramatic — and because it lets us feel superior to the past. The truth is far more impressive. Ordinary people, with copper tools and ramps and teamwork, built a mountain of stone so precise it still amazes engineers today, and they did it as free men. Strip away the whips of the movies, and the pyramid builders become something better than victims. They become us. Follow Embered Ages for more of the ancient world, brought to life by firelight."
