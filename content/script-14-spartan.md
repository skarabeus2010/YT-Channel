## VO — Kapitel (EN)
### CH1 Hook
> "They were the most feared warriors of the ancient world — men who chose death over retreat. But the Spartans weren't born tough. They were forged, through a childhood so brutal it would be unthinkable today. This is what it really took to become a Spartan."

### CH2 Birth
> "For a Spartan, the trials began at birth. According to the Greek writer Plutarch, every newborn was examined by the elders. A strong baby was allowed to live. A sickly one, it's said, was left to die. We should be careful — this comes from a source writing centuries later, and historians still debate how literally to take it. But it captures the Spartan ideal: from your first breath, your worth was measured by your usefulness to the state."

### CH3 The Agoge
> "At the age of seven, a Spartan boy was taken from his mother and thrown into the agoge — the state's brutal training system. He lived in barracks, was kept cold, hungry, and barefoot, and was toughened by constant competition and the lash. Strangest of all, boys were encouraged to steal food to survive — but savagely punished if they were caught. The lesson wasn't honesty. It was cunning, endurance, and never, ever getting caught."

### CH4 The helots
> "Spartan strength rested on a dark foundation. For every Spartan citizen, there were many times more helots — an enslaved people, the conquered Messenians, who farmed the land so Spartans could train for war full-time. The Spartans lived in permanent fear of a helot revolt. Each year they even declared war on their own slaves, and a secret band of young men, the krypteia, was sent out to murder any helot who seemed too strong or too defiant."

### CH5 The phalanx
> "On the battlefield, the Spartan fought as a hoplite, shoulder to shoulder in the phalanx — a wall of overlapping shields and spears. The individual didn't matter; the line did. A Spartan's great round shield protected the man beside him, so to drop it was the ultimate disgrace. A mother, legend says, handed her son his shield with the words: come back with this, or on it."

### CH6 Daily life
> "Even as an adult, a Spartan's life was not his own. He ate not at home but in a common mess, where the food was deliberately plain — most infamously a black broth of pork, blood, and vinegar that outsiders could barely stomach. Luxury was despised. Speech was kept short and sharp — the very word 'laconic' comes from Laconia, the Spartans' homeland. A Spartan was expected to say much with few words, and everything with his deeds."

### CH7 The women of Sparta
> "And then there were the women — who shocked the rest of Greece. Spartan girls were fed well, educated, and trained in athletics, because strong mothers were thought to bear strong sons. They could own property and speak their minds in public. While Athenian women were largely confined to the home, a Spartan woman ran the household and the estate while the men trained for war. Asked why Spartan women were the only ones who ruled men, a queen reportedly answered: because we are the only ones who give birth to men."

### CH8 Myth and reality
> "But the Spartan machine had a fatal flaw. The citizen body was tiny, and it shrank over time, while the enslaved majority simmered beneath it. Sparta could win immortal glory — three hundred Spartans holding the pass at Thermopylae — yet it could never replace its losses. In the end, the society built entirely for war was destroyed by war. The Spartans remind us that strength bought through cruelty is always, in the end, brittle. Follow Embered Ages for more of the ancient world, brought to life by firelight."
