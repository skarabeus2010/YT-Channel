## VO — Kapitel (EN)
### CH1 Hook
> "For five hundred years, one army conquered most of the known world. Not with magic or numbers, but with relentless discipline, engineering, and a soldier who could march twenty miles, build a fort, and fight a battle — all in the same day. This is what life was really like for a Roman legionary."

### CH2 Becoming a legionary
> "To join, you had to be a Roman citizen, and you signed away the next twenty-five years of your life. You swore a sacred oath of total obedience. In return came steady pay, plunder, and the promise of land when you retired — if you survived. Recruits were drilled until the brutal moves of battle became reflex. As one Roman writer put it, their drills were bloodless battles, and their battles were bloody drills."

### CH3 The gear
> "A legionary carried his world on his back. Body armour of iron strips, a heavy curved shield, a short stabbing sword, two javelins, plus a helmet — easily twenty to thirty kilograms. And that's before the marching pack: tools, stakes, cooking gear, and days of rations, slung on a pole over the shoulder. The soldiers joked bitterly that they were 'Marius' mules,' named after the general who made them carry it all."

### CH4 The march
> "A legion on the move was a machine. Twenty Roman miles in a day was routine, in step, in column. And at the end of it, they didn't just collapse. Every single night, in enemy territory, the army built a fortified camp from scratch — ditch, rampart, palisade, laid out to the same plan every time. An exhausted soldier still had to dig. The camp meant that Rome was never caught off guard."

### CH5 Food
> "Forget a diet of meat. A legionary ran mostly on grain — wheat ground into bread or boiled into a thick porridge called puls. Add cheese, bacon, beans, olive oil, and sour wine watered down into a drink called posca. It was simple, but it was reliable, and a Roman army was as much a logistics operation as a fighting force. Hungry soldiers lose wars; Rome made sure hers were fed."

### CH6 Battle
> "In battle, the legionary was not a lone hero — he was one cell of a body. The line advanced together, hurled a volley of javelins designed to bend on impact and jam an enemy's shield, then closed in with the short sword, stabbing from behind a wall of shields. They could lock those shields overhead into the famous 'tortoise' to cross open ground. Discipline, not fury, won Roman battles."

### CH7 Discipline and reward
> "Roman discipline was famously merciless. Fall asleep on watch, and you could be beaten to death by your own comrades. For mutiny, a unit might be decimated — one man in ten, chosen by lot, killed by the other nine. But there were carrots as well as sticks: medals, promotions, a share of loot, and cash gifts from a new emperor buying the army's loyalty."

### CH8 Life after the legion
> "Survive your twenty-five years, and Rome owed you. Veterans were paid off with money or a plot of land, often in a new colony on the frontier — a little Rome, full of old soldiers, that spread Roman roads, law, and language across the world. The legionary's greatest weapon, in the end, wasn't the sword. It was the shovel. Follow Embered Ages for more of the ancient world, brought to life by firelight."
