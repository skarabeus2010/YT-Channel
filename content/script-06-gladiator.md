## VO — Kapitel (EN)

### CH1 — Hook
> "Picture a Roman gladiator. A muscular slave, fighting to the death, while a bloodthirsty crowd screams for mercy or murder. It's one of history's most famous images — and almost all of it is wrong. The real gladiators were professional athletes. Many were celebrities. Most survived their fights. Some were even vegetarians. This is what it was actually like to live, train, and fight as a gladiator in ancient Rome — beyond the Hollywood myth."

### CH2 — Who they were
> "Most gladiators began as slaves, prisoners of war, or condemned criminals — sold to a man called a lanista, who ran a training school known as a ludus. The greatest, the Ludus Magnus, stood right beside the Colosseum, linked by an underground tunnel, with its own miniature arena. But not every gladiator was forced. Some were free men who volunteered, drawn by the fame, the crowds, and the prize money. To fight, they swore a brutal oath — to endure being burned, chained, beaten, and killed by the sword. They were the lowest of the low — and somehow, also stars."

### CH3 — The Barley Men
> "Life in the ludus was harsh, but surprisingly well-managed. Gladiators cost a fortune to train, so they were fed and cared for like prize athletes. They trained daily under specialist coaches called doctores, each an expert in one fighting style. And their diet? Mostly vegetarian — barley, beans, and lentils, in such quantity that Romans nicknamed them hordearii: 'barley men.' There was a reason. A layer of fat under the skin protected them from shallow cuts, letting a wound bleed dramatically without crippling them. After training, they're said to have drunk a tonic of plant ash and water — an ancient mineral supplement. We know this because scientists analysed gladiator bones, and read their diet in the chemistry."

### CH4 — Celebrities
> "And these men were famous. Gladiators were sex symbols and sporting heroes rolled into one. Their faces appeared on oil lamps and pottery; children played with gladiator figurines. Walls in Pompeii are scratched with their names and boasts — one fighter is praised as 'the heart-throb of the girls.' Roman women were said to keep tokens of their favourites; some even mixed gladiator sweat into cosmetics, believing it boosted beauty. For men with almost no legal rights, a champion could win money, adoration, and a kind of immortality."

### CH5 — The Types
> "Gladiators didn't just brawl — they fought in carefully matched styles, like characters in a game. The murmillo was a heavy fighter: tall shield, short sword, crested helmet. Against him might stand the retiarius — the 'net man' — lightly armoured, armed only with a trident and a weighted net, relying on speed to trap his opponent. Then came the Thracian, with his curved blade, and many more. Each pairing was a deliberate contrast: armour against agility, sword against net. The crowd had favourites, and the matchups were the whole point."

### CH6 — The Games & the salute myth
> "On the day of the games, the arena was pure theatre — trumpets, sand raked smooth, the editor, the sponsor, presiding over the crowd. You've probably heard the gladiators' famous greeting: 'Ave Caesar, those who are about to die salute you.' But there's no evidence gladiators ever said it. The phrase is recorded only once — spoken by condemned prisoners staging a mock sea-battle for the emperor Claudius. Professional gladiators, who trained for years and expected to survive, almost certainly never used it. It's a line history put in their mouths."

### CH7 — Fight to the death?
> "So did they really fight to the death? Usually — no. Gladiators were expensive. Training one took years and serious money, and if he died, the organiser had to pay his owner a fortune. So most fights ended not in death, but in mercy — missio — when a beaten fighter raised a finger to ask for his life. One historian puts a gladiator's odds of dying in any single match at roughly one in twenty. Fights to the death did happen — but they were the exception, reserved for special games, not the everyday rule."

### CH8 — The thumb myth
> "And the thumb? We all know the image: the emperor's thumb turns down, and the loser dies. But that picture comes from a painting — Jean-Léon Gérôme's, from 1872 — not from Rome. The Romans did use a thumb gesture to signal a gladiator's fate, but we genuinely don't know which way meant death. Up, down, sideways, or hidden in a fist — the sources don't agree. The most iconic 'fact' about gladiators is really a Victorian artist's guess."

### CH9 — Legacy
> "For those who survived long enough, there was a prize beyond money: the rudis — a wooden sword, the symbol of freedom, given to a gladiator who had earned his release. Many never reached it. Gladiator graveyards reveal men who died in their twenties, their bones marked by healed and fatal wounds. They were slaves and outcasts, fed like athletes, worshipped like stars — and remembered, two thousand years later, mostly through myths. Strip those myths away, and the real gladiator is somehow more human, and more fascinating, than the legend. Follow Embered Ages for more of the ancient world, brought to life by firelight."
